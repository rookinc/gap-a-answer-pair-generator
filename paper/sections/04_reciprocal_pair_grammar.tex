\section{Reciprocal pair grammar}

The local grammar has two halves.

First, reverse-partner rows answer by swapping \(B\) and \(C\):
\[
(A,B,C)\mapsto(A,C,B).
\]

Second, shared-\(B\) rows preserve the \(B\)-channel:
\[
B_{\mathrm{to}}=B_{\mathrm{from}}.
\]

The pair law couples them. The shared-\(B\) row receives its target \(C\) from the paired reverse-partner row:
\[
S.C_{\mathrm{to}}=R.B_{\mathrm{from}}.
\]

The reverse-partner row returns the shared-\(B\) source \(C\):
\[
R.B_{\mathrm{to}}=S.C_{\mathrm{from}}.
\]

In compact project language:

\[
\text{the witness is the nearest neighbor that answers back.}
\]

For this paper, that sentence is not a metaphorical claim. It means that the admissible local answer is encoded by a reciprocal neighboring row, and the pair is the unit tested by the selector.
